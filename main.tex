\begin{abstract}
Accurately judging the depth of cerebral vessels is central to neurosurgical planning and image-guided
navigation. We study the visualisation of cerebral angiography in video see-through (passthrough) Mixed
Reality (MR) on a standalone Meta Quest~3. Full direct volume rendering of a brain volume proved too costly
to sustain untethered while compositing over passthrough, so we render a segmented surface \emph{mesh} of
the prominent vasculature, which runs comfortably on-device and yields a cleaner, more interpretable
stimulus. The open question we target is whether depth-enhancement shaders improve depth judgement when
virtual anatomy is viewed against a real background --- a regime between 2D displays, where such shaders
clearly help, and immersive VR, where strong stereo and parallax make them wash out. We present a
standalone MR system that registers the vessel mesh to the real workspace and a rigorously controlled,
pre-registered protocol comparing three shaders --- Phong (reference), aerial-perspective fog, and
pseudo-chromadepth --- on an absolute in-scene target-placement task. The design holds the base surface
material and real-world occlusion constant across conditions, derives ground truth from the vessel
centreline, and decomposes placement error into view-axis (depth) and lateral components. We report
on-device rendering feasibility and discuss how the protocol is powered against the depth challenges
specific to passthrough MR. \textbf{[TODO: add one-sentence headline result once the feasibility pilot is
run.]}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Neurovascular interventions --- including the planning and clipping of aneurysms, the resection of
arteriovenous malformations (AVMs), and the management of arteriovenous fistulae (AVFs) --- depend on a
precise, three-dimensional understanding of how target vessels are situated within the surrounding
brain~\cite{kersten-oertel_augmented_2015-1}. A central difficulty is judging the \emph{depth} of a vessel
of interest: it is typically embedded within, and partially occluded by, other anatomy, so its position
relative to neighbouring vessels is hard to recover from the two-dimensional slices or flat desktop
renderings used in conventional review, which provide weak and often ambiguous depth
information~\cite{kersten-oertel_augmented_2015-1}. \textbf{[TODO: one sentence on the clinical consequence
of misjudging vessel depth, tied to collaborators' use case if possible.]}

Video see-through (passthrough) Mixed Reality (MR) is an appealing medium for this task: a head-mounted
stereoscopic display supplies binocular disparity and head-coupled motion parallax --- two of the strongest
depth cues --- while registering the vasculature to the clinician's real workspace keeps the physical
environment as context. Realising this on a \emph{standalone} headset, however, constrains the rendering.
Raymarched direct volume rendering (DVR) of a brain volume is bandwidth- and fragment-shader-bound, and
interactive medical DVR has been sustained only on PC-tethered or externally accelerated
systems~\cite{faludi_multivolume_2025}; in our own testing it could not hold interactive framerates
untethered while also compositing over the passthrough feed. We therefore render a segmented surface
\emph{mesh} of the prominent cerebral vasculature. The mesh runs comfortably within the standalone frame
budget, and --- because it dispenses with the transfer function whose opacity ramp otherwise entangles
occlusion and aerial perspective with the manipulation of interest --- yields a cleaner, more interpretable
stimulus for a depth-perception study.

The open question is whether \emph{depth-enhancement shaders} improve vessel depth judgement in this
setting. On 2D displays, cues such as aerial perspective (fog) and pseudo-chromadepth substantially improve
depth judgements of vascular data~\cite{kersten-oertel_evaluation_2014,ropinski_visually_2006}. In
immersive VR, by contrast, binocular stereo and motion parallax are so strong that shader differences
largely wash out~\cite{heinrich_estimating_2021,titov_effect_2021}. Passthrough MR sits between these
regimes and adds its own difficulties: the virtual mesh competes with a real, textured background, and the
passthrough imagery is geometrically distorted, reduced in resolution, and latent --- the depth and
occlusion problems long recognised in medical AR~\cite{macedo_occlusion_2023,adams_depth_2022}. Whether
depth-enhancement shaders help angiographic depth judgement here is therefore open, and clinically
consequential.

\noindent This paper makes three contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A \emph{standalone} (untethered) passthrough-MR system that registers a cerebral-vasculature mesh
        to the real workspace on the Meta Quest~3 and renders it, with real-world occlusion, within the
        standalone frame budget (Section~\ref{sec:system}).
  \item A rigorously controlled, pre-registered \emph{protocol} comparing three depth-enhancement shaders
        --- Phong, fog, and pseudo-chromadepth --- on an absolute in-scene target-placement task, with
        explicit confound controls and an error decomposition isolating the diagnostic depth axis
        (Section~\ref{sec:design}).
  \item An on-device \emph{feasibility} check confirming that mesh rendering sustains headset framerate
        over the passthrough pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:results}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\subsubsection{Depth cues for vascular visualisation.}
For rendered vasculature, local (Phong) surface shading is the conventional illumination baseline; on a
surface mesh its normals are well defined, so it is a fair reference rather than a source of gradient
noise. Added depth cues improve on it. Aerial perspective (fog) encodes distance as
desaturation/attenuation, and pseudo-chromadepth --- a depth-to-colour ($\text{red}\rightarrow\text{blue}$)
mapping introduced for angiography~\cite{ropinski_visually_2006} --- is consistently rated among the
strongest static cues for vascular data~\cite{kersten-oertel_evaluation_2014}; it has been adapted
specifically to vascular \emph{surface} models~\cite{behrendt_combining_2017}, the setting we adopt. These
results motivate our three-condition set: Phong (reference), fog, and pseudo-chromadepth. Depth-cue
comparisons are well developed for surface renderings on conventional and VR
displays~\cite{hombeck_evaluating_2022}, but have not been transferred to registered, passthrough-MR
viewing of cerebral vasculature.

\subsubsection{Depth perception in immersive VR and the ceiling effect.}
Much of that evidence comes from 2D displays. In stereoscopic VR with free head movement, disparity and
motion parallax are themselves powerful cues, and several studies report that shader choice then matters
little: depth judgements of vascular models were more precise in VR than on desktop yet barely
distinguished shading methods~\cite{heinrich_estimating_2021}, interactive cues had little impact on
relative-depth perception for angiographic volumes in VR~\cite{titov_effect_2021}, and, once camera motion
is permitted, depth perception depends more on scene content than on the chosen
technique~\cite{lesar_evaluation_2024}. This ceiling effect motivates our move to passthrough MR and
requires that difficulty be raised deliberately. Methodologically, 3D-pointing studies show that error
along the view (depth) axis behaves differently from lateral error and is the axis on which depth cues
act~\cite{teather_pointing_2011,batmaz_effect_2022}, motivating an error decomposition rather than a single
3D distance.

\subsubsection{Depth perception in passthrough augmented and mixed reality.}
Passthrough viewing changes the depth problem rather than easing it. The depth of virtual content overlaid
on a real scene is systematically misperceived, modulated by display type, shadows, and relative
position~\cite{adams_depth_2022} and by object shape, colour, and luminance~\cite{do_effects_2020}; without
correct occlusion, virtual structures appear to float in front of real ones, a long-standing, only
partially solved problem~\cite{macedo_occlusion_2023,shah_occlusion_2012}. These effects are acute for
medical overlays, and depth-enhancing vascular visualisation has been pursued specifically for
neurovascular AR~\cite{kersten-oertel_volume_2013,kersten-oertel_augmented_2015}. The closest prior work
compares depth cues for vasculature but on desktop or tethered VR and with relative-depth or detection
tasks~\cite{kersten-oertel_evaluation_2014,titov_effect_2021}; we extend that comparison to
\emph{standalone passthrough MR} and to an \emph{absolute} in-scene target-placement task whose error is
decomposed into depth and lateral components, with the mesh registered to the real workspace and real-world
occlusion enabled but held constant across conditions.

\section{System}\label{sec:system}

\subsection{Vessel Mesh and Base Material}\label{sec:system-mesh}
The stimulus is a segmented triangle \emph{mesh} of the prominent cerebral vasculature, rendered as an
opaque surface; the same mesh, skeletonised to its \emph{centreline}, provides the ground truth used in the
task (Section~\ref{sec:design-task}). Choosing a mesh over full DVR is what makes the system run untethered
(Section~\ref{sec:system-perf}) and removes the transfer function as a confound: with no opacity ramp,
occlusion is simply correct surface occlusion and is held constant across conditions. A single base
material --- surface colour and Phong lighting response --- is shared by all conditions, so each condition
adds only its distance encoding on top of an identical baseline. \textbf{[TODO: dataset --- modality,
source, and number of cases --- to be supplied; state mesh extraction (segmentation + surface
reconstruction) once fixed.]}

\subsection{Depth-Enhancement Shaders}\label{sec:system-shaders}
The three conditions share the base material and differ only in the added depth cue: \emph{Phong}, standard
local surface shading, which serves as the reference; \emph{fog}, an explicit parameterised
depth$\rightarrow$desaturation applied post-shading and disabled in the other conditions; and
\emph{pseudo-chromadepth}, a depth-dependent red$\rightarrow$blue colour
mapping~\cite{ropinski_visually_2006,behrendt_combining_2017}.

\subsection{Passthrough Compositing and Registration}\label{sec:system-passthrough}
The system is built in Unreal Engine 5.7.3. The mesh is drawn over the Quest~3 passthrough feed using
Meta's Passthrough API and world-locked to the workspace with a Meta Spatial Anchor on the participant's
table, so it stays fixed as the participant moves. Real-world occlusion is resolved with the Quest~3 Depth
API, so the participant's hands and nearby objects correctly occlude the mesh; this occlusion is enabled
and held constant across all conditions (Section~\ref{sec:design-conditions}).

\subsection{Standalone Rendering on Quest~3}\label{sec:system-perf}
Unlike raymarched DVR, an opaque surface mesh is inexpensive to rasterise, so the system comfortably
sustains the headset display rate alongside the passthrough and depth pipelines rather than the reduced
target a standalone volume raymarch would impose. Measured performance is reported in
Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\section{Study Design and Protocol}\label{sec:design}

\subsection{Task and Ground Truth}\label{sec:design-task}
The mesh is world-locked to the workspace with a Meta Spatial Anchor and viewed through the passthrough
cameras, appearing as a solid object on the participant's table. On each trial the participant views the
vasculature under one shader condition and indicates the perceived 3D location of a designated target
vessel by positioning a virtual marker with a hand controller. Ground truth is the vessel \emph{centreline}
node nearest the intended landmark, obtained by skeletonising the segmented mesh; this gives an unambiguous
anatomical point even though the rendered vessel has a finite radius. \textbf{[TODO: confirm input modality
(controller ray vs.\ direct grab) and marker form (point vs.\ small sphere).]}

\subsection{Conditions and Confound Controls}\label{sec:design-conditions}
The single manipulated factor is the depth-enhancement shader, applied within subjects: Phong, fog, and
pseudo-chromadepth (Section~\ref{sec:system-shaders}). The overriding control is that a \emph{single base
material} --- surface colour and Phong response --- and correct opaque occlusion are held constant across
all conditions, so each shader changes only how distance is encoded, not the underlying surface or its
occlusion~\cite{kersten-oertel_evaluation_2014,titov_effect_2021}. Fog is an explicit, parameterised
depth-to-desaturation function \emph{disabled} in the non-fog conditions, so the fog condition isolates an
added distance encoding; in passthrough it blends toward a fixed reference colour rather than the variable
real background, so it is not confounded with scene content. Stereo separation, motion parallax, the
passthrough path, and real-world occlusion are held identical across conditions; manipulating occlusion
handling is left to future work (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Procedure and Difficulty Calibration}\label{sec:design-procedure}
After a short training block, each participant completes the three conditions in a counterbalanced order
(e.g.\ a Latin square), with several targets per condition spanning a range of depths.
\textbf{[TODO: fix trial counts and timings after piloting.]} Two considerations shape the procedure.
First, because stereo and parallax can still produce a strong ceiling
effect~\cite{heinrich_estimating_2021,titov_effect_2021}, difficulty is raised deliberately: targets sit
deep in the vasculature, behind other vessels so occlusion ordering is ambiguous (self-occlusion), and in
visually crowded regions known to degrade depth perception~\cite{lesar_evaluation_2024}. Second, because
free head movement recovers depth from parallax, viewing is constrained (e.g.\ a brief static-viewing
window or system-driven rotation rather than unrestricted head movement) and a response deadline
discourages extended viewpoint change. Head pose is logged throughout as a covariate. The exact depths,
crowding, viewing-window duration, and deadline are fixed in a pilot that places performance off both floor
and ceiling before the main run.

\subsection{Error Metrics and Outcomes}\label{sec:design-metrics}
For each placement, the 3D error vector between the marker and the ground-truth centreline node is
decomposed --- using the headset pose at placement --- into a component along the view (camera) axis, the
\emph{depth} error, and an orthogonal \emph{lateral} component. This separation is standard in 3D pointing
because depth is the axis on which the cues act~\cite{teather_pointing_2011,batmaz_effect_2022}, and only
the depth component is diagnostic of a shader's effect. The \emph{signed} depth error is also recorded, to
detect the systematic depth compression characteristic of head-mounted displays. The pre-registered primary
outcome is the (unsigned) depth error; lateral error, signed depth error, and completion time are secondary.

\subsection{Participants, Power, and Analysis}\label{sec:design-analysis}
The perceptual sample will be drawn from a non-clinical population (e.g.\ computer-science and engineering
students), on the rationale that the core depth judgement is a basic visual-system function and that prior
work found similar novice/expert cue rankings for it~\cite{kersten-oertel_evaluation_2014}. Prior AR/VR
experience and anatomy familiarity are collected as covariates, and an optional small expert arm allows a
clinical-validity check. \textbf{[TODO: set target $n$ from an a-priori power analysis once the pilot
yields effect-size and variance estimates.]} The analysis is pre-registered: depth error is modelled with a
linear mixed-effects model with shader condition as a fixed effect and a per-participant random intercept
(random slope where supported), with target depth and logged covariates as controls. \textbf{[TODO: name
the primary contrast(s) and the multiple-comparison correction.]} Because the literature predicts a
possible null, the design is powered for the difficulty-manipulated contrast, and a null is treated as
informative (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\section{Discussion, Limitations, and Conclusion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsubsection{Interpreting the anticipated outcomes.}
Because passthrough MR does not trivially solve depth --- the mesh competes with a textured background, the
imagery is distorted and latent, and depth relative to the real scene is easily misjudged ---
depth-enhancement shaders have more room to help here than in immersive VR, where native cues dominate and
shader differences wash out~\cite{heinrich_estimating_2021,titov_effect_2021}. A reliable improvement in the
\emph{depth} component of placement error would be directly useful for MR neuronavigation, whereas a null
would itself be informative: even in this harder regime, and with a physical anchor supplying a strong
external depth reference, added shader cues would not improve vessel depth judgement. The pre-registered
analysis and the depth-versus-lateral decomposition make either outcome interpretable, and the difficulty
calibration (Section~\ref{sec:design-procedure}) keeps the comparison sensitive.

\subsubsection{Limitations.}
Several limitations bound the design. Rendering an opaque vessel \emph{mesh} rather than the full volume is
what makes the system run untethered and cleanly isolates the shader manipulation, but it abstracts away the
surrounding tissue and the semi-transparent embedding that characterise the clinical view; the study
therefore speaks to depth judgement of the vascular \emph{surface} in situ rather than of vessels seen
through translucent parenchyma. The subtlest remaining confound is the overlap between fog and any residual
distance shading; we drive fog from an explicit depth function that blends toward a fixed reference colour
(Section~\ref{sec:design-conditions}) and report its parameters in full. Passthrough adds its own
confounds --- geometric distortion, limited resolution, latency, and residual registration error --- which
we standardise across conditions and log. Because the mesh is anchored to the workspace rather than the
patient, the study speaks to MR \emph{planning and inspection} rather than in-situ intraoperative
navigation, and the anchor may aid localisation of the vasculature as a whole more than of an internal
target vessel. Finally, a non-clinical sample bounds \emph{perceptual}, not clinical, validity; because
anatomically meaningful targets could let clinicians infer depth from anatomical
priors~\cite{kersten-oertel_evaluation_2014}, we use plausible but non-canonical target points and include
an optional expert arm.

\subsubsection{Conclusion and outlook.}
We have presented a standalone passthrough-MR system that renders a registered cerebral-vasculature mesh on
the Meta Quest~3 and a rigorously controlled, pre-registered protocol for evaluating whether
depth-enhancement shaders improve depth judgement in this setting, together with an on-device feasibility
check. The immediate next step is to run the pre-registered study; beyond it, the clearest extensions are to
make real-world occlusion a manipulated factor rather than a fixed baseline --- the depth cue most specific
to MR --- and to move from a workspace anchor toward in-situ overlay on a patient phantom.
