\begin{abstract}
Judging the depth of cerebral vessels is central to neurosurgical planning, and depth-enhancement
shaders such as aerial perspective (fog) and pseudo-chromadepth measurably improve such judgements on
2D displays. Whether they help in video see-through (passthrough) Mixed Reality (MR) --- where virtual
anatomy competes with a real background, but binocular disparity and motion parallax are also available
--- is open. We report a within-subjects study ($n=12$) on a standalone Meta Quest~3. Participants
viewed a cerebral-vasculature mesh embedded in a brain shell, opened by a virtual probe that cut an
aperture into the shell, and performed a two-stage task: identify a designated marker among three
candidates, then place a marker at its remembered location once it was hidden. We first establish that
the task is depth-limited: placement error along the view axis was approximately $2.4\times$ larger than
per-axis lateral error, in all 12 participants without exception (Wilcoxon $W=0$, $p=0.00049$, the
minimum attainable at $n=12$). Against that sensitive task, no shader produced any detectable effect on
any performance outcome. Subjectively, however, participants diverged sharply: 7 of 10 single-choice
respondents named pseudo-chromadepth the most helpful shader (binomial $p=0.020$, exploratory), none
chose fog, and Phong was rated worst on all six workload subscales. Users preferred a cue that did not
measurably help them. We argue this dissociation arises because viewing was unconstrained: with free
head movement over world-locked anatomy, motion parallax supplies the depth information the shaders
would otherwise carry, leaving no headroom for a shader to act in. The study is exploratory and not
pre-registered.

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Neurovascular interventions --- including the planning and clipping of aneurysms, the resection of
arteriovenous malformations (AVMs), and the management of arteriovenous fistulae (AVFs) --- depend on a
precise, three-dimensional understanding of how target vessels are situated within the surrounding
brain~\cite{kersten-oertel_augmented_2015-1}. A central difficulty is judging the \emph{depth} of a
vessel of interest: it is typically embedded within, and partially occluded by, other anatomy, so its
position relative to neighbouring vessels is hard to recover from the two-dimensional slices or flat
desktop renderings used in conventional review, which provide weak and often ambiguous depth
information~\cite{kersten-oertel_augmented_2015-1}.

Video see-through (passthrough) Mixed Reality (MR) is an appealing medium for this task: a head-mounted
stereoscopic display supplies binocular disparity and head-coupled motion parallax --- two of the
strongest depth cues --- while registering the vasculature to the clinician's real workspace keeps the
physical environment as context. On 2D displays, depth-enhancement shaders such as aerial perspective
(fog) and pseudo-chromadepth substantially improve depth judgements of vascular
data~\cite{kersten-oertel_evaluation_2014,ropinski_visually_2006}. In immersive VR, by contrast,
stereo and parallax are so strong that shader differences largely wash
out~\cite{heinrich_estimating_2021,titov_effect_2021}. Passthrough MR has been argued to sit between
these regimes: the virtual anatomy competes with a real, textured background, and the passthrough
imagery is geometrically distorted, reduced in resolution, and latent --- the depth and occlusion
problems long recognised in medical AR~\cite{macedo_occlusion_2023,adams_depth_2022}. On that reasoning,
shaders should have more room to help here than in VR.

We tested that expectation and did not find it. This paper reports a within-subjects study ($n=12$) on a
standalone Meta Quest~3 comparing three shaders --- Phong (reference), fog, and pseudo-chromadepth ---
on an absolute in-scene placement task. Our contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item A \emph{standalone} passthrough-MR system that renders a cerebral-vasculature mesh inside a
        brain shell, world-locked to the workspace, with a probe-driven aperture that reveals the
        vasculature in situ (Section~\ref{sec:system}).
  \item A demonstration that the placement task is genuinely \emph{depth-limited}: view-axis error is
        approximately $2.4\times$ per-axis lateral error, in every participant
        (Section~\ref{sec:results-validity}). This matters because a null on an insensitive task is
        uninformative; ours is not.
  \item The central finding --- a \emph{dissociation}. No shader affected any performance outcome, yet
        participants strongly preferred pseudo-chromadepth and rated Phong as most demanding on every
        workload subscale (Sections~\ref{sec:results-perf}--\ref{sec:results-subj}).
  \item An account of why: with unconstrained viewing of world-locked anatomy, motion parallax already
        supplies the depth information, leaving no headroom for a shader
        (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\subsubsection{Depth cues for vascular visualisation.}
For rendered vasculature, local (Phong) surface shading is the conventional illumination baseline; on a
surface mesh its normals are well defined, so it is a fair reference rather than a source of gradient
noise. Added depth cues improve on it. Aerial perspective (fog) encodes distance as
desaturation/attenuation, and pseudo-chromadepth --- a depth-to-colour ($\text{red}\rightarrow\text{blue}$)
mapping introduced for angiography~\cite{ropinski_visually_2006} --- is consistently rated among the
strongest static cues for vascular data~\cite{kersten-oertel_evaluation_2014}; it has been adapted
specifically to vascular \emph{surface} models~\cite{behrendt_combining_2017}, the setting we adopt.
These results motivate our three-condition set. Depth-cue comparisons are well developed for surface
renderings on conventional and VR displays~\cite{hombeck_evaluating_2022}, but have not been transferred
to registered, passthrough-MR viewing of cerebral vasculature.

\subsubsection{Depth perception in immersive VR and the ceiling effect.}
Much of that evidence comes from 2D displays. In stereoscopic VR with free head movement, disparity and
motion parallax are themselves powerful cues, and several studies report that shader choice then matters
little: depth judgements of vascular models were more precise in VR than on desktop yet barely
distinguished shading methods~\cite{heinrich_estimating_2021}, interactive cues had little impact on
relative-depth perception for angiographic volumes in VR~\cite{titov_effect_2021}, and, once camera
motion is permitted, depth perception depends more on scene content than on the chosen
technique~\cite{lesar_evaluation_2024}. Our result is best read as an extension of this ceiling effect
rather than a contradiction of it (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}). Methodologically, 3D-pointing studies
show that error along the view (depth) axis behaves differently from lateral error and is the axis on
which depth cues act~\cite{teather_pointing_2011,batmaz_effect_2022}, motivating an error decomposition
rather than a single 3D distance --- and, in our case, supplying the validity check of
Section~\ref{sec:results-validity}.

\subsubsection{Depth perception in passthrough augmented and mixed reality.}
Passthrough viewing changes the depth problem rather than easing it. The depth of virtual content
overlaid on a real scene is systematically misperceived, modulated by display type, shadows, and
relative position~\cite{adams_depth_2022} and by object shape, colour, and
luminance~\cite{do_effects_2020}; without correct occlusion, virtual structures appear to float in front
of real ones, a long-standing, only partially solved
problem~\cite{macedo_occlusion_2023,shah_occlusion_2012}. These effects are acute for medical overlays,
and depth-enhancing vascular visualisation has been pursued specifically for neurovascular
AR~\cite{kersten-oertel_volume_2013,kersten-oertel_augmented_2015}.

\subsubsection{Preference versus performance.}
That users \emph{prefer} a visualisation is not evidence that it helps them. Prior work on vascular
depth cues has reported subjective ratings alongside performance and found pseudo-chromadepth rated
highly~\cite{kersten-oertel_evaluation_2014}, and the general possibility that preference and
performance dissociate is well known in visualisation evaluation. Our study is, to our knowledge, the
first to report such a dissociation for depth-enhancement shaders in passthrough MR, and --- because we
first establish that the task is depth-sensitive --- to be able to attribute the null to the viewing
regime rather than to an insensitive measure.

\section{System}\label{sec:system}

\subsection{Anatomical Model}\label{sec:system-mesh}
The stimulus is a surface mesh of the prominent cerebral vasculature, segmented from a single-patient
time-of-flight MR angiography (TOF-MRA) scan in 3D Slicer~\cite{fedorov_3d_2012} over pre-defined
anatomical regions of interest, restricted to the vasculature clearly apparent in the scan. The surface
was generated with Slicer's Model Maker and then cleaned and smoothed, and affinely registered to the
ICBM-152 template brain~\cite{fonov_unbiased_2011}, which supplies the outer brain shell. Smoothing
matters for the comparison at hand: it yields well-defined surface normals, so the Phong reference
reflects genuine local shading rather than segmentation-boundary noise. The model is therefore a \emph{composite}: one patient's vessels in a population-average
brain, not patient-specific anatomy. Because the registration is affine rather than deformable, the
vessels sit in approximately --- not exactly --- their anatomical position relative to the template. Both
choices are adequate for a perceptual study, where the requirement is plausible and internally
consistent geometry rather than clinical fidelity, but they bound the clinical reading of our results
(Section~\ref{sec:limitations}). Rendering an opaque surface mesh rather than performing
direct volume rendering is what makes the system run untethered: raymarched DVR of a brain volume is
bandwidth- and fragment-shader-bound and has been sustained only on PC-tethered or externally
accelerated systems~\cite{faludi_multivolume_2025}. A mesh also removes the transfer function as a
confound --- with no opacity ramp, occlusion is simply correct surface occlusion, held constant across
conditions.

\subsection{Depth-Enhancement Shaders}\label{sec:system-shaders}
The three conditions share a single base material --- surface colour and Phong lighting response --- and
differ only in the added distance encoding: \emph{Phong}, standard local surface shading, the reference;
\emph{fog}, an explicit parameterised depth$\rightarrow$desaturation applied post-shading and disabled in
the other conditions; and \emph{pseudo-chromadepth}, a depth-dependent red$\rightarrow$blue colour
mapping~\cite{ropinski_visually_2006,behrendt_combining_2017}. Because the base material and occlusion
are identical across conditions, each shader changes only how distance is encoded, not the underlying
surface. In passthrough, fog blends toward a fixed reference colour rather than the variable real
background, so it is not confounded with scene content.

\subsection{Probe and Aperture}\label{sec:system-probe}
The vasculature is enclosed by the opaque brain shell and is not visible from outside it. Bringing a
hand-held virtual probe near the shell cuts a small aperture into it, which remains open, revealing the
vessels within; the probe may be advanced inside the shell. This deliberately mimics the constrained
view of a neurosurgical approach: a small opening, a target beneath it, and an instrument reaching
through. The tip of the probe is the interaction point for both stages of the task
(Section~\ref{sec:design-task}).

\section{Study Design}\label{sec:design}

\subsection{Task}\label{sec:design-task}
On each trial, three small white markers were displayed on the vessel surface at differing depths.
Participants were instructed: \emph{``Select the marker in the middle of the three dots.''} They
selected one with the probe tip and confirmed with a button press, at which point \textbf{the markers
were hidden} and they placed a marker at the remembered location of the one they had selected. The
placement stage is therefore \emph{open-loop}: participants could not see the target while placing, and
so could not converge on it by visual alignment. This is what makes placement a depth judgement rather
than a visuomotor tracking task, and it is confirmed empirically in
Section~\ref{sec:results-validity}. Ground truth is the position of the designated marker; error is
decomposed, using the headset pose at placement, into a component along the view axis (\emph{depth}) and
an orthogonal \emph{lateral} component~\cite{teather_pointing_2011,batmaz_effect_2022}.

\subsection{Conditions and Design}\label{sec:design-conditions}
The single manipulated factor was the shader, applied within subjects. Each participant completed 18
trials: six target locations, each presented once under each of the three shaders. Shader is thus
crossed with target by construction and cannot be confounded with target identity. Presentation order
was shuffled per participant (12/12 distinct orders; no order effect detectable, Spearman
$\rho=+0.09$, $p=0.23$). All participants saw the same target locations, re-anchored per session.

The model is rendered at life size: the marker cloud spans $22.9 \times 2.7 \times 17.0$\,cm, with a
median nearest-neighbour marker spacing of $2.68$\,cm, and participants viewed targets from a mean of
$48$\,cm (range $16$--$72$\,cm). Because viewing was unconstrained, the depth separation of the three
candidate markers was itself determined by the viewpoint each participant adopted: it varied from
$0.2$ to $18.9$\,cm across trials (median $4.0$\,cm), with all three markers falling within $2$\,cm of
one another in depth on 36 of 180 trials. The difficulty of the selection stage was therefore not held
constant, a point we return to in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Excluded Trials}\label{sec:design-exclusions}
One target block (trials 15--17, three trials per participant) is excluded from all analyses, leaving
$12 \times 15 = 180$ trials in a balanced 5/5/5 design. The ground-truth point for that block is not a
plausible target: it lies $8.14$ units from the nearest other marker in the stimulus, against a median
nearest-neighbour marker spacing of $2.68$ units --- roughly $3\times$ off-cloud. Because the model is
rigidly re-anchored per session and distances are invariant under rigid transforms, these two figures
are \emph{identical to two decimal places for all twelve participants}, which identifies the cause as a
single authoring fault in the stimulus rather than a per-session error. The block accordingly shows
$0/36$ selection accuracy across every participant --- a deterministic outcome, not a behavioural one.
The exclusion is on apparatus grounds and is independent of the shader comparison; the block was
uniformly faulty across all three conditions.

\subsection{Participants and Procedure}\label{sec:design-participants}
Twelve participants were drawn from a non-clinical population, on the rationale that the core depth
judgement is a basic visual-system function and that prior work found similar novice/expert cue rankings
for it~\cite{kersten-oertel_evaluation_2014}. Five wore glasses. Most were VR novices (seven had used a
headset only once or twice; two weekly, one monthly, two yearly). Six reported no familiarity with brain
anatomy, three some, one detailed. Sessions took approximately 25 minutes.

\subsection{Analysis}\label{sec:design-analysis}
\textbf{This study was not pre-registered.} The emphasis on view-axis depth error as the outcome of
interest was fixed in advance in consultation with the supervisor, and follows the 3D-pointing
literature in which depth is the axis on which cues act~\cite{teather_pointing_2011,batmaz_effect_2022};
but there is no timestamped public registration, and we therefore report all outcomes as
\emph{exploratory} and privilege none of them inferentially. All outcomes examined are reported below,
including those that are null. Because $n=12$ and the per-participant distributions are not assumed
normal, we use Friedman omnibus tests with Wilcoxon signed-rank post-hocs under Holm correction. We note
throughout that with $n=12$ the smallest two-sided $p$ attainable by any distribution-free test is
$2/2^{12}=0.00049$; any smaller value would be an artefact of a parametric assumption rather than
something the data can support.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

\subsection{The task is depth-limited}\label{sec:results-validity}
A null result is only informative if the task could have detected an effect. We therefore first ask
whether placement error is dominated by depth at all, or is merely isotropic hand noise --- which, having
no preferred axis, could not be improved by any depth cue.

We decomposed each error vector in a view-aligned orthonormal frame (one axis along the view direction,
two perpendicular) and compared RMS error along each. This makes no distributional assumption. Depth RMS
error was $0.429$ against a per-axis lateral RMS of $0.193$: a ratio of $2.37$ (median $2.41$). The
effect is unanimous --- \emph{all twelve} participants show depth error exceeding lateral error
(Wilcoxon signed-rank $W=0$, $p=0.00049$, which is exactly the floor at $n=12$). The evidence here is
the unanimity and the magnitude; the $p$-value merely confirms that twelve-for-twelve is not a coin
flip.

Depth is thus roughly $2.4\times$ harder than the lateral axes in this task, exactly as the pointing
literature predicts~\cite{teather_pointing_2011,batmaz_effect_2022}. The task has a large, depth-specific
error component available for a shader to reduce.

\subsection{But participants strongly preferred chromadepth}\label{sec:results-subj}
Subjective responses diverge sharply from performance. Asked which shader they found most helpful, 7 of
the 10 participants who named a single condition chose pseudo-chromadepth and 3 chose Phong;
\emph{none} chose fog (binomial against $1/3$: $p=0.020$). Counting the two further participants who
named several conditions, chromadepth was named by 9 of 12.

\textbf{This item is damaged and we report it with that caveat.} The response options were edited
partway through data collection, so one condition appears under two labels (merged before tallying), and
the item permitted multiple selections rather than forcing a choice. It is also one significant result
among approximately thirteen tests in an unregistered study, and would not survive Bonferroni correction
across that set ($0.05/13 = 0.0038$). We therefore treat it as exploratory and suggestive, not
confirmatory.

Workload responses corroborate the direction. On a seven-point TLX-style
instrument~\cite{hart_development_1988} (higher is worse on every subscale; the Performance subscale

significance --- Effort is closest ($p=0.063$) --- and the subscales are correlated, so this is
corroborating rather than independent evidence. But the direction is unanimous.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsubsection{A dissociation, and why it arises.}
Our participants preferred a cue that did not measurably help them. The task was demonstrably
depth-limited ($2.4\times$, unanimous), so the null is not an artefact of an insensitive measure; and
the preference is consistent across two instruments that point the same way. The natural explanation is
that \emph{viewing was unconstrained}. Participants moved freely and took as long as they wished:
head-position range was a median $2.08\times$ the full extent of the marker cloud, and selection times
reached $235$\,s on some trials. Against world-locked anatomy, free head movement makes motion parallax
veridical and abundant --- and parallax is precisely a depth cue. The shaders were therefore competing
against a cue that had already resolved the depth, and had no headroom in which to act.

This makes our result an \emph{extension} of the VR ceiling
effect~\cite{heinrich_estimating_2021,titov_effect_2021,lesar_evaluation_2024} rather than a
contradiction of it. The expectation that passthrough MR would restore a role for shaders --- because the
imagery is degraded and the background cluttered --- was not borne out: when parallax is freely
available, passthrough behaves like VR in this respect. It is worth being explicit that this is not the
result we expected, and that our own framing (Section~\ref{sec:intro}) predicted otherwise.

Why, then, the preference? A plausible reading is that chromadepth reduces the \emph{effort} of a
judgement that participants could make regardless --- consistent with Effort being the subscale closest to
significance and with Phong scoring worst on all six. A cue can make a task feel easier without making
it more accurate. For system designers this is not nothing: a visualisation that lowers perceived
workload at no cost to accuracy may still be worth adopting. But it should not be sold as an accuracy
aid on this evidence.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}
Several limitations bound these conclusions, and the first is the most important.

\emph{Viewing was unconstrained.} We did not restrict head movement or impose a response deadline.
Because free viewing supplies parallax, our design cannot separate ``shaders do not help in passthrough
MR'' from ``shaders do not help \emph{when parallax is available}''. We believe the latter, and the
head-movement data support it, but the study cannot decide between them. This is the single change we
would make first, and it is arguably the clinically relevant one: a surgeon at a fixed vantage over a
craniotomy cannot orbit the anatomy, so the parallax-limited regime --- not ours --- is the one that
matters most.

\emph{The selection stage is not a depth measure.} The instruction ``select the marker in the middle of
the three dots'' does not specify \emph{middle in depth}, and the app's ground truth defines
``middle'' in the model's coordinate frame rather than along the view axis. The two come apart: the
designated marker is the true view-axis middle on only 65\% of trials, and on 60\% of trials a second
marker also satisfies the model-frame criterion, so the item often has more than one defensible answer.
Compounding this, unconstrained viewing meant the depth separation of the three candidates was set by
the participant's own vantage rather than by the design, ranging from $0.2$ to $18.9$\,cm
(Section~\ref{sec:design-conditions}); on some trials the three markers were effectively coplanar in
depth, leaving no depth ordering to recover. Selection accuracy is accordingly reported descriptively
only, and should not be read as a depth-ordering judgement. The placement stage, which is our actual outcome, is unaffected by this: it
requires only that the participant aims at the marker they chose.

\emph{The sample is small and the study exploratory.} With $n=12$ and no pre-registration, we can claim
only that we \emph{could not detect} a difference, never that none exists; the confidence intervals
readily admit effects that would matter clinically. The preference result in particular is one
significant test among many and needs independent confirmation.

\emph{The scene is fully virtual.} Both the brain shell and the vasculature are rendered; no physical
object was present. The world-lock supplies parallax but not a real surface at a known distance, so our
results do not speak to the case where virtual anatomy is registered to a physical phantom or patient.

\emph{The model is a composite.} One patient's vessels registered to a template brain is adequate for
perceptual work but is not patient-specific anatomy, and the study speaks to depth judgement of a
vascular surface in situ rather than of vessels seen through translucent parenchyma.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
We built a standalone passthrough-MR system that renders cerebral vasculature inside a probe-openable
brain shell on a Meta Quest~3, and used it to compare Phong, fog, and pseudo-chromadepth on an
absolute depth-placement task ($n=12$). The task is demonstrably depth-limited --- view-axis error is
roughly $2.4\times$ per-axis lateral error, in every participant --- yet no shader improved any
performance outcome. Participants nonetheless preferred pseudo-chromadepth and rated the Phong reference
as the most demanding condition on every workload subscale. We attribute the null to unconstrained
viewing: against world-locked anatomy, free head movement supplies the parallax that leaves shaders no
headroom.

The clear next step follows directly from that account: constrain the viewpoint, and test whether
shaders recover a measurable benefit when parallax is unavailable. Registering the anatomy to a physical
phantom, rather than presenting it fully virtually, is the natural companion change.
